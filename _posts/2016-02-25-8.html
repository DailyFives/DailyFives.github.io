--- layout: post title: "Today's Five: Feb 25, 2016" date: 2016-02-25 22:30:00 +0530 categories: dailyfives, team, coding, customer support, startups published: true --- 1. What Google Learned From Its Quest to Build the Perfect Team - by CHARLES DUHIGG

Charles Duhigg is the author of the famous book "The Power of Habit". In this except (which is still long form) from his upcoming book, he explains about a new research which reveals surprising truths about why some work groups thrive and others falter.


2. Reactions: Not everything in life is Likable - by Geoff Teehan

Facebook's recent announcement about Reactions meant there would have been so many things that would have happened behind the scenes. Geoff Teehan, who is the Product Design Director at Facebook, explains the process behind designing Reactions and the decisions that were made along the way.


3. WHY WON’T THIS WORK?! Coding Angry for Fun and Profit - by Reto Meier

Reto Meier, who is an Android Developer Advocate at Google, writes about why doing things the wrong way can be the right way when building apps. In the article, there is also a video that shows a 16 minute screencast of Reto Meier coding a basic functionality for an Android app and how he approaches it. This is a really fantastic article and a video. He asks:

Writing good code is hard, but if building something the simplest, easiest way can still be frustrating, what effect does the extra cognitive load of trying to build it the right way have, when you’re just trying to experiment?

4. The Ultimate Guide to Customer Support - by Zapier Team

Although not technically an article, this is an amazingly useful free book. You will be able to read it online for free and you can get a pdf copy by subscribing to their list.

We all know the importance of customer support and how vital a part it plays in the success of a business. So everyone should read this and if you are a founder, have your team read this.


5. HubSpot’s Playbook for Going From Startup to Scale-up - by Brian Halligan

CEO of Hubspot, Brian Halligan, explains about the process of going from startup to scale-up. He tells you about the four stages: 1. Getting Product Market Fit 2. Getting the Math to Work 3. Getting the Math to Scale 4. Getting Saturated
